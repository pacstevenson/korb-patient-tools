What Foundation Is

Foundation is the entry point into the Functional Health & Longevity Program. It is one peptide active at a time — not a stack, and never two agents running simultaneously. It exists for patients who want to try peptide therapy at the lowest cost and complexity point before considering Gateway or Peak.

  • Provider selects one agent from the formulary based on the patient's primary goal.
  • All four Foundation agents start on the same day (Week 1) — no staggering. Staggering is a Gateway/Peak feature only.
  • Baseline labs are required, same as Gateway and Peak — no prescription is issued at the baseline visit.
  • Patients are not locked into one agent forever. At each 16-week cycle renewal, the patient may continue the same agent or switch to a different Foundation agent — only one active at a time. Switching is normal, not an escalation.
  • If a patient wants to run two agents at the same time, that's a Gateway or Peak conversation — Foundation does not support simultaneous combination.

Athlete / WADA Disclosure & Compounding Safety

Athlete / WADA — Hard Stop

Competitive or tested athletic status is a hard stop for Foundation. Every agent in this program is prohibited, or must be treated as prohibited by default, under World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) rules.

Do not initiate any Foundation agent for a patient who discloses competitive or tested athletic status. Document that this restriction was discussed regardless of which agent was under consideration.

Compounding & Sourcing Safety

All Foundation agents are compounded peptides, not FDA-approved commercial products.

Requirement: per-lot third-party purity and endotoxin testing for all compounded material used in this program.

This is a standing sourcing requirement, not a per-patient counseling point. Confirm current lot testing status with the pharmacy relationship owner (Premier/Greenwich) rather than re-verifying at each prescription.

Agent Monograph — Sermorelin (GRF 1-29 / GHRH Analog)

Definition. Synthetic 29-amino-acid peptide corresponding to the active N-terminal fragment of endogenous GHRH. Formerly FDA-approved as Geref. A GH secretagogue, not exogenous growth hormone.

Mechanism. Binds the GHRH receptor on anterior pituitary somatotrophs → cAMP → pulsatile endogenous GH release. Because release stays pulsatile and under somatostatin/IGF-1 negative feedback, it preserves the physiologic GH axis — the key safety advantage over exogenous rHGH. Downstream GH drives hepatic IGF-1, mediating protein synthesis, lipolysis, connective-tissue repair, and slow-wave sleep.

Clinical evidence. Genuine human endocrine data (former FDA approval). Raises IGF-1 into youthful-physiologic range in GH-decline states with documented improvements in body composition, sleep quality, and subjective recovery. Not RCT-validated specifically for wellness/recovery use in healthy adults — counsel accordingly.

Side effects. Generally well tolerated. Injection-site reactions (most common), flushing, transient headache. Dose-dependent GH-class effects if IGF-1 runs high: fluid retention, arthralgia, carpal-tunnel-type symptoms, insulin resistance.

Contraindications — Absolute: Active malignancy or remission < 5 years; known hypersensitivity; uncontrolled diabetes/pre-diabetes (HbA1c > 5.7%) or significant insulin resistance; untreated hypothyroidism; active proliferative retinopathy; severe untreated sleep apnea; pregnancy/lactation.
Cautions: History of malignancy; elevated baseline IGF-1; concurrent GH-axis therapy.

Medication interactions. Glucocorticoids blunt GH response; adequate thyroid status required; somatostatin analogs (octreotide) directly antagonize; anticholinergics may blunt release; antidiabetic agents may need adjustment.

Agent Monograph — CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin

Definition. Combination of two GH-axis secretagogues. CJC-1295 (modified GRF 1-29, no DAC, ~30-min half-life) is a GHRH analog; Ipamorelin is a selective ghrelin-receptor agonist (GHS-R1a). Co-activation of both receptors produces synergistic GH release greater than either alone.

Mechanism. CJC-1295 binds the GHRH receptor → cAMP → pulsatile GH release under normal feedback. Ipamorelin agonizes GHS-R1a, initiating a GH pulse and suppressing somatostatin — notably without cortisol/prolactin/ACTH elevation seen with older secretagogues. This selectivity is its defining clinical feature.

Clinical evidence. Human pharmacokinetic and endocrine data exist for both components. CJC-1295 trials show dose-dependent, sustained GH/IGF-1 increases (mean IGF-1 rising ~1.5–3x) in healthy adults. Benefits are extrapolated from GH-axis physiology, not dedicated combination RCTs.

Side effects. Injection-site reactions, transient flushing, headache, water retention. Ipamorelin: occasional increased hunger. Dose-dependent GH-class effects at high IGF-1.

Contraindications — Absolute: Active malignancy or remission < 5 years; known hypersensitivity; pregnancy/lactation; uncontrolled diabetes/pre-diabetes (HbA1c ≥ 5.7%); untreated hypothyroidism; active proliferative retinopathy; severe untreated sleep apnea.

Medication interactions. Glucocorticoids blunt GH response; thyroid sufficiency required; somatostatin analogs antagonize; ghrelin-pathway appetite effect may compound with other orexigenic agents.

Agent Monograph — BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157)

Definition. Synthetic 15-amino-acid pentadecapeptide, a partial sequence derived from a protein found in human gastric juice. No approved human pharmaceutical form exists.

Mechanism (preclinical). Upregulates VEGFR2–Akt–eNOS signaling to drive angiogenesis; modulates the nitric oxide system (cytoprotective); promotes tendon fibroblast outgrowth/survival via FAK-paxillin signaling; upregulates GH-receptor expression in tendon fibroblasts; gut cytoprotection; anti-thrombotic, neuroregulatory, neuroprotective; accelerates bone healing.

Clinical evidence. Robust tendon/ligament/muscle/bone healing acceleration in rodent models. No published human RCTs. The recovery rationale is extrapolated entirely from preclinical work — counsel patients plainly on this evidence gap.

Side effects. Human safety data essentially absent; wide therapeutic window in rodent toxicology. Anecdotal: transient fatigue, lightheadedness, injection-site reactions. Long-term human safety unknown.

Contraindications — Relative (theoretical): Active malignancy (pro-angiogenic VEGF mechanism); pregnancy/lactation (no data).

Medication interactions. Theoretically offsets NSAID/corticosteroid GI toxicity. Possible additive growth-signaling effect if a patient has recently used a GH secretagogue.

Agent Monograph — GHK-Cu (Copper Tripeptide-1)

Definition. Naturally occurring tripeptide (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine) coordinated with a copper(II) ion. Present in human plasma (~200 ng/mL at age 20, falling to ~80 ng/mL by age 60). Injectable form is off-label/compounded.

Mechanism. Delivers copper as a cofactor for lysyl oxidase (collagen/elastin crosslinking) and superoxide dismutase (antioxidant defense). Stimulates collagen, elastin, glycosaminoglycan, and proteoglycan synthesis. Gene-modulatory: resets thousands of genes toward a "younger" expression profile. Pro-angiogenic; supports nerve outgrowth and wound contraction.

Clinical evidence. Best-supported uses are dermatologic/regenerative (skin elasticity, wound healing, photoaging) with controlled human topical data. Systemic/injectable claims are extrapolated from mechanism and topical data — not from injectable RCTs.

Side effects. Injection-site irritation, redness/swelling, headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, skin irritation/rash, rare allergic reaction. Unknown long-term safety for systemic use.

Contraindications — Absolute: Pregnancy/lactation; active malignancy without clearance; unexplained mass/lesion/systemic symptoms; known Wilson's disease or copper metabolism disorder; known hypersensitivity to copper-containing products.

Medication interactions. No major known interactions; caution with concurrent investigational peptides (additive angiogenic effect).

Escalation / Discontinuation (Foundation-wide)

⛔ Hard stop — discontinue immediately

Active or suspected malignancy identified at any point; hypersensitivity/anaphylaxis-type reaction; pregnancy identified; significant unexplained systemic symptoms.

⚠ Flag and reassess

IGF-1 rising above target range (Sermorelin, CJC/Ipam); HbA1c/glucose drifting upward; copper/ceruloplasmin trending up (GHK-Cu); no meaningful benefit after trial period; patient requesting two agents at the same time — a Gateway/Peak conversation. Switching to a different Foundation agent at the next cycle is normal, not a flag.
